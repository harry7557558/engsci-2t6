CIV102 Bridge Design `design_nd`

The bridge design consists of three segments symmetrical about the middle span. The middle one is thicker than the two at the sides due to glue joints. Glue joints are closer to the end for a lower bending moment and curvature.

The body of the bridge is an inverted trapezoid. There are two layers of matboard at the top, with flanges extending beyond the top of the trapezoid to increase the height of centroidal axis and therefore decrease y in the equation My/I and increase compressive failure load. Components are designed to strengthen the middle span and the supports of the bridge.

The bottom and sides of the trapezoid are folded. Inspired by the winning group last year, we decide to glue the edges between paddings instead of using folded tabs for the top of the trapezoid and diaphragms. These parts are mainly subjected to compression and are less likely to fail due to tension and shear. Intuitively, folding tabs involves deforming the matboard, making it more likely to buckle.

This bridge design has 7 diaphragms in the middle span. Since the analysis shows shear buckling is not the main cause of the bridge's failure, we consider there is no need for extra diaphragms at the ends. The cross section of the middle of the bridge will likely shrink vertically due to bending moment and therefore have a decreased second moment of area. Diaphragms are placed in the middle to resist such deformation.

Analysis based on the CIV102 course shows the bridge will first fail due to compression in the midspan. However, intuition tells us the glue joint between beam segments may be the weakest part. The bridge may also crush at the ends due to reaction force.

How the matboard will be cut

The matboard is divided into rectangles. Bottom left is (0, 0), top right is (1016, 813). Light yellow is support. Light blue is diaphragm. White is beam. Gray is unused region. All units are in mm. One pixel in this diagram is 1 mm.

On a desktop device, you can mouse hover a rectangle to read the numbers. The second row contains the coordinates of the corners of a rectangle on the matboard. The third row contains the width and height of the rectangle on its own coordinate system. Note that rectangles may be rotated. A sequence of point coordinates in the rectangle's coordinate system representing the cut or fold is followed (if cut or fold exists.)

When marking and cutting the matboard, label each rectangle before cutting it off so we don't mess it up. The bridge components are packed very compactly. Make sure you make no mistake when marking and cutting the board - there is no room for a mistake. We have extra materials left, and we may consider using them to strenghten the endpoints of the bridge that support the reaction force.
